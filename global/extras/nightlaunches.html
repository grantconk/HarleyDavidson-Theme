Night Launches

Since the start of the shuttle program, there have been 31 night launches, totalling about one-fourth of the 117 shuttle missions to date (as of May 8, 2007).

STS-116, in December of 2006, marked the first night launch since 2003. After the tragic loss of orbiter Columbia and her crew in that year, night launches were ruled out; NASA managers wanted the full visibility of daylight to make it easier to spot any damage on the ascending orbiter. NASA has since determined that "Return to Flight" reengineering efforts over the intervening years have made it safe enough for night launches to resume.

For the complete list of all shuttle missions, see Shuttle Missions on the NASA web site.

Notes
  1. NASA uses a "15 Minute Rule" to determine night launches and landings. By this rule, a launch or landing event is classified as a night event if it happens in the span of time that begins 15 minutes after local sunset and ends 15 minutes before local sunrise. It's not clear how it's handled in the case when launch or landing time is in the minute that is exactly 15 minutes before sunrise or after sunset.
  2. Sunrise and sunset times obtained from U.S. Naval Observatory tables.
  3. Sunrise and sunset times are all listed as EST (Eastern Standard Time), as provided in the USNO tables. Some launch times are listed as EDT (Eastern Daylight Savings Time). To compare the two times, subtract one hour from the EDT to get the equivalent EST value, then compare.
  4. NASA's Shuttle Launch Archive lists STS-65 and STS-77 as night launches. Examination of other sources shows these are actually day launches. Similarly, it classifies the following missions as day launches, when they should be classified as night launches: STS-61C, STS-44, STS-86 and STS-103. Examination of other sources shows these are actually night launches.
  5. STS-41G launched exactly 15 minutes before sunrise (truncating times to the whole minute). It's not clear how NASA classified this, but we decided to list it as a night launch.
  6. Some NASA launch photos look like nighttime when they really aren't, because they're shot at low exposure to capture detail in and around the bright rocket exhaust plumes. NASA engineers need this detail to judge the performance of the rockets and rocket motors. If these photos were shot at normal exposure, the plumes from the Solid Rocket Boosters would look solid white and wash out all detail. Shooting at low exposure darkens the whole picture, making the plume area more visible and the skies look like night.
  7. Thanks to everyone at NASAspaceflight.com (especially DaveS, Ben, Ben E, and dsl) for helping assemble the final list of night launches.
Postscript

The launch of STS-123 (Endeavour), on March 11, 2008, has pushed the number of night launches up to 32 out of 122 total launches.
